## 3. Cognitive and rhetorical style: how Nick thinks and persuades

### Reasoning approach

**Experience-first, then data**: Typically leads with personal story or observation, then backs with metrics:
> *"Our cold open rate is 49%... Even better? Our cold response rate is 22%."*

**Shows work methodically**: Walks through reasoning steps in podcasts:
> *"Column one is I could create with an open AI search... Okay, and it goes and finds all their URLs. Alright, now I want you to tell me which of these URLs also includes HubSpot..."*

**Contrarian but evidence-based**: Challenges conventional wisdom with specific examples:
> *"There is no one who, behind closed doors, and I'm doing it here in public, who actually thinks our history of attribution has actually been useful for anything other than explaining why we're spending the money that we're spending to the CFO. I don't believe in it."*

### Persuasion patterns

**Primary appeal**: **Logos** (logic) delivered through **Pathos** (emotion/frustration)

**Social proof deployment**:
- Names specific marketing luminaries (Jay Baer, Ann Handley, Dave Gerhardt)
- Credits advisors and team members by name
- Uses client results as proof points

**Analogy domains**:
- **Aviation** (Air Traffic Control branding): "marketing captains, demand gen navigators, digital co-pilots"
- **Animals** for content examples: "You open up an email about giraffes... then there's a link about a hippopotamus"
- **Food/kitchen**: "If you haven't wrapped your arms around the customer's problem and been like, swimming in their tears, you should definitely get out of the kitchen"

- Keep sentences short and punchy
- Include specific examples, not abstract claims
- Avoid buzzword bingo ("synergies," "touch base")
- Show you've done homework on HIM specifically, not just his company

- Lead with a concrete observation or problem

- Be direct, not diplomatic

**Trust-building**:
- Demonstrate competence through specificity

- Give before asking â€” share insight, not pitch
